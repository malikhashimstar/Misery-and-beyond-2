I. The Pattern

Why do you think we suffer?

Look around you—there are people. Just think about it. There are people you see who are always suffering, and you keep hoping that their suffering will end. You might even paint a picture in your mind that this will pass soon—but for them, it never does.

Life keeps giving them shocks, like earthquakes striking at intervals. For a moment, there is happiness, and then suddenly, everything falls quiet again.

You may feel that their life will eventually turn out well, because you are not seeing what they are truly going through. Because for them, it has never been easy—it has always been hard.

I knew a person, and I thought the same—that this was only for a brief moment, that he would suffer for a while, and eventually the universe would reward him with something he had never even imagined.

I always believed in that idea—that life has its ups and downs, and that, in the end, you will find the stability you have always wanted.

At least, that is what I thought.

But over time, something began to feel uncertain.

Not loudly, not all at once—
but slowly, in ways that are difficult to explain.

You begin to notice patterns you once ignored. The same people, the same kind of suffering, repeating itself without any clear reason.

And you start to wonder—
is life really balanced the way we believe it to be?

Because if it were, then why do some people seem to carry more than their share, while others move through life untouched by the same weight?

Why does relief come easily to some, while for others, it remains distant—almost theoretical?

It is not that suffering exists—that much is obvious. It is that it does not seem to be distributed equally.

And once you begin to see that, the idea of "everything will eventually be okay" no longer feels like truth.

It starts to feel like something we tell ourselves—
not because it is certain,
but because we need it to be.

II. A Good Man

And then, this person I used to know died.

Just a few days before his death, I met him. We spoke about life. I asked him how things were going, and in a weary voice, he said only a few words—nothing is going well.

I hesitated, but I still asked him about his life—how it had been, what he had been going through. And I came to know many things about his life.

But even then, the only thought in my mind was that this would end well. That he would not suffer forever. That he would eventually find peace. That he would find stability.

And that belief kept me at ease—even after knowing that his life was not going well, even after knowing that he was suffering.

Because somewhere in my mind, I held on to the idea that everything, in the end, becomes okay. That whatever he was going through was only temporary.

And so, I chose not to worry too much.

And then, the worst happened.

My belief lost its meaning. No matter how I think about him now, no matter what I imagine for him—it changes nothing. Because he is no longer there to see any of it.

His life has dissolved into what cannot be returned.

I will not go into the details of who he was, but I must say this—he was a good man.

And to me, he deserved change. He deserved to be happy. He deserved peace.

I do not know what the criteria are for a peaceful life, but if goodness is one of them, then he was surely eligible.

But it did not happen that way.

And perhaps, we say—we do not know what Allah has written for us. This is not a complaint against God. It is something else.

It is the quiet realization that maybe we do not understand life the way we think we do.

We live as if life will explain itself to us.
As if it will eventually justify what it takes away.

And so, we take it lightly.
We assume there is time.
We assume there is balance.

But perhaps… life is not as simple, or as forgiving, as we make it out to be.

I am not denying that there is balance in life. For some people, life does seem balanced—they go through ups and downs, and eventually, they find a kind of stability.

But you know what I call them? I call them lucky.

Because even after all the disruption, their lives still move toward something meaningful—something worth holding on to.

And at the same time, we cannot deny another reality—that there are people who are not as lucky.

In my own understanding, we can never truly predict what life has prepared for us. You may be working hard, doing everything right, expecting a stable or even a comfortable life—

and still, things change.

Sometimes they turn so suddenly, so violently,
that nothing you imagined remains at the end.

III. The Absence of Love

And there is something else that cannot be ignored.

Love.

Because misery is not only shaped by what happens to us—it is also shaped by what is missing. And one of the deepest absences a person can feel is the absence of being loved.

To be unseen, unwanted, or left out of meaningful human connection—this becomes a quiet form of suffering.

You may endure hardship, uncertainty, even loss—but when you feel loved, something within you remains intact.

You do not feel entirely alone.

You do not feel completely out of place.

There is still a sense that you matter to someone.

And perhaps that is what every human being is searching for—
not perfection,
not even happiness,
but to feel wanted.

And when that is missing, even a stable life can begin to feel empty.

We live in a world where love, in many ways, has lost its meaning. It is no longer something that is simply felt—it is something that is judged, filtered, and often reduced to appearances.

People decide whom to give their attention to based on how you look, how you present yourself, how you fit into what is considered acceptable.

This is not limited to love between two people—it extends to almost every human interaction.

If you look good, if you carry yourself well, if you meet certain expectations, people stay. If you do not, they begin to drift away.

We have moved past what nature intended, and created a world where connection is no longer rooted in understanding, but in selection.

And in such a world, those who do not meet these unspoken conditions are left unseen—not because they do not feel, but because they are not chosen.

And that, too, becomes a form of misery.

IV. To Not Turn Away

And there is another absence that deepens it—the absence of belonging. The absence of being seen. The absence of being able to show the world what you are capable of, and having no one who truly notices.

A life begins to lose its meaning not only because of suffering, but because it lacks what is needed to sustain it. It needs a seed of love—something small, but enough to plant hope.

Without that, even existence begins to feel empty.

I am reminded of Frankenstein. If such a being were real, what would be his greatest misery? Not that he is called a monster. Not that he cannot escape his condition. But that he has no companion—and may never have one.

Because when a being has no one to share its existence with—no one who understands, no one who stays—even life itself begins to feel like a burden.

How is it that a person, whose most basic instinct is to survive, can lose the will to live when someone they love is gone? How is it that the absence of one person can make existence itself feel unbearable?

Perhaps survival is not only of the body, but of something deeper.

And when that breaks, the body alone is not always enough to keep a person here.

Because man is built to survive—
but not alone.

And perhaps the problem is not that we do not see suffering, but that we do not stay with it long enough to understand it.

We look, we acknowledge, and then we move on. Because it is easier that way.

But what if, for a moment, we did not look away?

What if we allowed ourselves to feel what they feel—
not from a distance,
but from within?

Because the truth is—it could be us.

And if that is so, then the least we owe to those who live this reality…

is to not turn away from it.
